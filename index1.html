------------------------SAVE ARAVALI------------------------

The Supreme Court settled on a uniform definition of the Aravalli hills and ranges, and paused the grant of fresh mining leases inside its areas spanning Delhi, Haryana, Rajasthan and Gujarat.

The Save Aravalli Hills movement has gained renewed urgency following a recent Supreme Court ruling that adopted a uniform definition of the Aravalli hills. While the judgment paused fresh mining leases, concerns remained that the new definition could exclude large portions of this ancient mountain system from legal protection.

The resulting public outcry reflects deeper anxieties about ecological degradation, water insecurity, and worsening pollution across North India. The debate around the Aravalli Ranges is therefore not only legal, but also environmental and developmental.

WHY THE SAVE ARAVALI MOVEMENT MATTERS

The growing momentum of the Save Aravalli movement reflects public recognition that the fate of the range is closely linked to regional sustainability.Degradation of the Aravallis directly affects water availability, air quality, and climate resilience in North India. Urban centres such as Delhi, Gurugram, and Jaipur are particularly vulnerable to these impacts.

The movement has also highlighted the need for transparent governance and science-based policymaking. Calls for safeguarding biodiversity, maintaining ecological connectivity, and ensuring sustainable livelihoods resonate with India's broader environmental commitments. By engaging citizens, legal institutions, and policymakers, the movement underscores that environmental protection is a shared responsibility.

MINERALS, MINING, AND ENVIRONMENTAL DEGRADATION

The Aravalli region is rich in minerals such as limestone, marble, sandstone, copper, zinc, and tungsten. These resources have historically made it a mining hub, contributing to regional economies and construction needs. However, decades of excessive quarrying have severely degraded the landscape.

Since the early 1990s, environmental regulations have sought to restrict mining to sanctioned projects. Despite this, illegal and unregulated mining expanded in several parts of Haryana and Rajasthan. Forest cover declined, hill slopes were flattened, and groundwater tables fell sharply. Air quality also worsened, particularly in the National Capital Region, as dust from mining and stone-crushing units added to existing pollution sources.

In 2009, the Supreme Court imposed a blanket ban on mining in districts such as Faridabad, Gurugram, and Mewat in Haryana. While this was a landmark intervention, enforcement challenges persisted on the ground and resulted in continuation of illegal extraction in many areas.

CENTRAL EMPOWERED COMMITTEE RECOMMENDATIONS

Recognising the long-term ecological risks of unregulated mining, the Central Empowered Committee proposed a science-based and multi-layered strategy for protecting the Aravallis.

Key elements of the approach include comprehensive scientific mapping of the Aravalli range across all States, allowing for a shared and credible knowledge base. The Committee also called for macro-level environmental impact assessments of mining activities to capture cumulative effects rather than project-by-project impacts.

Crucially, it recommended strict prohibition of mining in ecologically sensitive zones such as wildlife corridors, aquifer recharge areas, water bodies, and protected habitats. Until mapping and assessments are completed, no new mining leases or renewals are to be permitted. The Committee further highlighted the need to tightly regulate stone-crushing units that contribute significantly to air pollution.

These measures underline an approach of ecosystem-based management, a principle strongly echoed in the Save Aravalli discourse.

CONCLUSION

The debate around the Aravallis is ultimately about the kind of development India chooses to pursue. The Aravalli Mountain Range is not merely a repository of minerals, but a living system that sustains water, climate, and biodiversity for millions. The recent Supreme Court ruling and subsequent public mobilisation have brought this reality into sharp focus.

Protecting the Aravallis through informed policy, responsible mining practices, and long-term ecological restoration is essential for regional resilience. The Save Aravalli movement, therefore, represents more than a response to the Supreme Court ruling, it is a call to safeguard a natural heritage that underpins the future of North India.
